Objective

Turn a confusing pricing page into a conversion machine.

NBI's subscription page was generating calls to customer service — not subscriptions. The page was dense, visually overwhelming, and asked visitors to make too many decisions before they even understood what they were buying.

I identified the problem on my own, built the case for a redesign, and executed the new page from strategy through launch — no brief handed to me, no agency involved.

The Before

Six friction points killing the conversion.

The original page had good intentions but broke down on execution. Here's what I flagged before touching a single pixel.

1
Pointless state selector
A dropdown to filter by state — but the difference between states was only a handful of courses. Not a deciding factor. Pure noise that added a decision where none was needed.
2
Hidden billing toggle
The monthly/annual toggle was visually buried. Customer service was fielding constant calls from people who didn't see it and assumed they could only pay monthly. A UX failure disguised as a feature.
3
Tiny, forgettable pricing
Prices were displayed in a small, gray font inside a dense comparison table. On a page asking someone to spend $99–$129/month, the price should be the hero — not an afterthought.
4
Spreadsheet-style hero
The above-the-fold section looked like a feature matrix, not a product page. Row after row of checkmarks gave no emotional context for why a customer should care.
5
Marketing mush mid-page
A large section with generic benefit statements that read like filler copy. Nothing specific, nothing memorable, nothing that advanced the decision to subscribe.
6
Zero social proof
No testimonials. No client logos. No names attached to any claim. Visitors were expected to convert on product description alone — with nothing and no one to vouch for it.

My Process

Identify. Simplify. Build.

No agency handoff. No formal brief. I spotted the problem, connected it to business impact, and proposed the redesign — then saw it through from strategy to publish.

01

Audit & root cause analysis

I mapped the page against what a visitor actually needs to convert — clear pricing, a confident value statement, and social proof. Every element that didn't serve one of those three goals became a candidate for removal.

02

Reduce decisions, increase clarity

I removed the state selector entirely. I pulled the billing toggle out into a prominent, clearly labeled visual switcher so it couldn't be missed. Pricing became large, confident, and front-and-center — because hiding the price doesn't build trust, it builds hesitation.

03

Rebuild the comparison section

I replaced the spreadsheet-style table with a cleaner card layout focused on key differentiators — not a feature dump. The goal was to help a visitor self-select, not overwhelm them into paralysis.

04

Replace filler copy with real proof

The generic benefits section came out. In its place: credibility signals — real firm names, real numbers (40+ years, 100,000+ reviews, 1,000+ new courses/year), and a testimonials section that gave prospective subscribers something to believe.

05

Build & publish

I built the full redesigned page and took it through to launch — clean layout, improved mobile experience, and a page that finally reflected the quality of the product it was selling.

Outcome

The page finally did its job.

After launch, the data reflected what the design logic predicted: fewer distractions, more conversions. Removing friction works.

↓ Bounce
Bounce rate dropped after launch — visitors were staying and engaging instead of leaving
↑ Subs
Subscription volume increased meaningfully — the clearer the page, the easier the decision
↓ CS Calls
Fewer billing confusion calls after the toggle was redesigned as a clear visual switcher
Takeaway

Good marketing pages remove decisions. Great ones make the right decision feel obvious.

This project wasn't assigned to me — I identified the problem, built the case, and executed the solution independently. The most impactful marketing isn't always new campaigns or bigger budgets. Sometimes it's fixing the page that's already in front of your customers.
